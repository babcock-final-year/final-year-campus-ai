## Overview

Babcock University is a private, co-educational institution of higher learning located at Ilishan-Remo, Ogun State, Nigeria. It is fully owned and operated by the Seventh-day Adventist (SDA) Church and registered under the laws of the Federal Republic of Nigeria. The university was among the first three private universities to receive recognition from the Federal Government of Nigeria in 1999, alongside Igbinedion and Madonna Universities. It does not receive government grants.

The university is named after **David Caldwell Babcock (1854–1932)**, an American Adventist missionary who pioneered Seventh-day Adventist work in Nigeria by establishing the first Adventist elementary school at Erunmu, southwest Nigeria, in 1914.

## Historical Timeline

### 1914 — Adventist Educational Beginnings
David C. Babcock established the first Adventist elementary school in Nigeria at Erunmu. Over subsequent decades, a network of elementary and secondary schools spread across the country under Adventist mission leadership.

### 1954 — Appeal for a Tertiary Institution
At the General Conference session of 1954, the West African Union Mission formally appealed to the world Seventh-day Adventist Church for the establishment of a degree-granting tertiary institution to meet the growing need for trained pastors, teachers, and leaders in West Africa.

### 1959 — Founding as the Adventist College of West Africa (ACWA)
A site at Ilishan-Remo, Ogun State, was selected following feasibility studies led by the West African Union Mission Committee under Roger Coon. On **September 17, 1959**, the institution opened its doors under the name *The Adventist College of West Africa (ACWA)* with an initial enrollment of seven ministerial students. **Grover C. Winslow** was appointed as the first president. ACWA initially offered a three-year diploma programme before seeking approval to offer degree programmes.

### 1966
ACWA awarded its first set of Bachelor of Arts degrees in Theology under its own name.

### 1967–1970 — Nigerian Civil War
Enrollment declined significantly during the civil war as students from Eastern Nigeria withdrew and new admissions were disrupted. ACWA's relationship with the government remained cordial, however.

### 1975 — Name Change to Adventist Seminary of West Africa (ASWA)
Under President Percy Paul, the institution was renamed *Adventist Seminary of West Africa (ASWA)* in response to the political climate, which no longer favoured private degree-awarding institutions. The seminary status protected the institution from the threat of government takeover. That same year, ACWA made history as the **first institution in Nigeria** to sign an affiliation agreement with **Andrews University**, Berrien Springs, Michigan, USA. ASWA graduates received Andrews University degrees and were accepted into the National Youth Service Corps.

### 1981–1983
Roland L. McKenzie became president in 1981. Student unrest led to a leadership change, and **Adekunle A. Alalade** was appointed president, a position he held until 1999.

### 1988
ASWA reached a new milestone by affiliating with the Seventh-day Adventist Theological Seminary of Andrews University to offer the **M.A. in Pastoral Ministry (by extension)**. The M.A. in Religion was later added in 1990.

### 1999 — Charter as Babcock University
On **May 10, 1999**, after sustained efforts to obtain autonomous university status, the Federal Government of Nigeria issued a charter for the institution to operate as **Babcock University**. The Certificate of Registration No. 002 was presented at a ceremony at the National Universities Commission (NUC) in Abuja. The university was formally inaugurated on **June 17, 1999**. It welcomed its premier intake of **1,006 students** on **September 13, 1999**. Adekunle A. Alalade became the first Vice-Chancellor.

### 2000
The maiden matriculation ceremony was held on **January 28, 2000**, presided over by Prof. Jan Paulsen, General Conference President of the Seventh-day Adventist Church.

### 2001
The **Benjamin S. Carson Sr. School of Medicine** was established — the first Adventist medical school in Africa. It was named after world-renowned Adventist pediatric neurosurgeon Dr. Benjamin S. Carson Sr.

### 2006–2015 — Rapid Expansion under Vice-Chancellor Makinde
Under **Prof. J.A. Kayode Makinde**, the university grew substantially. A School of Law was established at a satellite campus in Iperu-Remo. The College of Health Sciences (later the Benjamin S. Carson Sr. College of Health and Medical Sciences) was developed. The university established itself as the **only Adventist university in Africa** offering programmes in law and medicine.

### 2014
The **Laz Otti Memorial Library**, donated by Dr. Alex Otti, was commissioned. The library was renamed from the earlier *Adekunle Alalade Library*.

### 2015
**Prof. Ademola S. Tayo** succeeded Makinde as Vice-Chancellor/President in December 2015.

### 2016
Full accreditation was achieved for both the medicine and law programmes. The university received the **Global Brand Award** in 2015 and 2016. The first 13 medical graduates were inducted into the profession in January 2017.

### 2025
**Prof. Afolarin Olutunde Ojewole** succeeded Tayo as Vice-Chancellor/President in December 2025.

### Present
Babcock University now hosts over 13,000 students across two colleges and multiple schools, offering undergraduate and postgraduate programmes. It remains the largest Seventh-day Adventist institution of higher learning in the world.
